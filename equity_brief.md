# Mental Health Equity Brief
## Provincial Mental Health Outcomes — CCHS Analysis
**Prepared for:** Provincial Mental Health Director
**Data source:** Statistics Canada CCHS PUMF | 5,000 respondents | All provinces
**Analysis date:** 2024

---

## Purpose

This brief summarises a cross-provincial analysis of mental health outcomes, condition prevalence, and unmet need using Statistics Canada CCHS data. It identifies which populations are most underserved and recommends three specific investments.

---

## The National Picture

**66.8%** of Canadians report good or excellent mental health. That means one in three does not.

**13.8%** report unmet mental health need — they needed care and did not get it. Among Indigenous Canadians, this rises to 53.8%.

**29.6%** report at least one diagnosed mental health condition. Anxiety disorders (12.0%) now exceed depression (9.4%) as the most prevalent condition — a shift that has accelerated since 2015 and reflects both genuine increases and improved diagnosis rates.

---

## The Three Gaps That Matter Most

### Gap 1: Atlantic provinces are the worst-served region in Canada

| Province | Good MH % | vs National |
|----------|----------|------------|
| NB | 61.0% | -5.8pp |
| NL | 62.2% | -4.6pp |
| NS | 63.1% | -3.7pp |
| **National** | **66.8%** | — |

NB, NL, and NS rank in the bottom three nationally. The Atlantic gap is not explained by population demographics alone. These provinces have the fewest psychiatrists per capita in the country, the highest rural population proportions, and limited virtual care infrastructure.

When people cannot access care in rural Atlantic Canada, they go without. The unmet need rate in these provinces reflects a structural access failure, not a lack of demand.

### Gap 2: Income is the strongest predictor of poor mental health

| Income Group | Good MH % | Poor MH % |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| Under $30K | 51.7% | 48.3% |
| $30K–$60K | 64.0% | 36.0% |
| $60K–$100K | 72.0% | 28.0% |
| Over $100K | 80.4% | 19.6% |

The gradient is linear and stark. A Canadian earning over $100K is 1.5x more likely to report good mental health than one earning under $30K. This is not a surprise — financial stress, housing insecurity, and inability to pay for private therapy all drive poor mental health outcomes in lower-income groups.

The critical insight is that this group has the least access to the care it needs most. Low-income Canadians are less likely to have private insurance, less able to pay out-of-pocket, and more likely to face waitlists of 12+ months for publicly funded services.

### Gap 3: Indigenous Canadians face a 4.6x unmet need gap

Indigenous respondents report good mental health at 54.6%, compared to 67.4% for non-Indigenous respondents — a 12.8 percentage point gap. More significantly, Indigenous unmet mental health need (53.8%) is **4.6 times higher** than non-Indigenous need (11.7%).

This is not a gap that mainstream service expansion will close. The evidence from across Canada is consistent: Indigenous Canadians do not access mainstream mental health services at adequate rates, and when they do, outcomes are often poor because services are not culturally safe.

The only interventions that have shown sustained impact are Indigenous-designed, Indigenous-delivered, and community-based.

## Condition Prevalence

| Condition | Prevalence | Unmet Need Among Those Diagnosed |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| Anxiety | 12.0% | High |
| Depression | 9.4% | High |
| Substance Use | ~8% | Very high |
| PTSD | ~5% | High |
| Bipolar | ~3% | High |

Anxiety overtaking depression as the most prevalent condition is a significant shift. Online CBT programs — which have strong evidence for mild-to-moderate anxiety — represent the highest-value, lowest-cost intervention available at scale.

## Three Investments That Would Make a Difference

### 1. Atlantic virtual care expansion

**Who:** NB, NL, NS

**What:** Remove referral requirements for provincial health card coverage of virtual mental health services, including synchronous therapy and asynchronous CBT platforms.

**Why this works:** Virtual care removes the geography barrier that drives Atlantic underservice. Published evidence from BC's BounceBack program and Ontario's Internet-based CBT initiatives shows clinically significant outcomes at a fraction of in-person delivery costs.

**Estimated reach:** If 20% of current unmet need in Atlantic provinces accessed virtual services, approximately 180,000 additional Canadians would receive annual mental health support.

### 2. Low-income subsidised counselling — community based, not hospital based

**Who:** Canadians earning under $30K

**What:** Fund community-based, subsidised counselling programs delivered through community health centres, not hospital outpatient departments.

**Why community, not hospital:** Hospital-based mental health referrals carry average wait times of 12–18 months in most provinces. Community health centres serve patients within weeks. The barrier is funding, not capacity.

**The key design principle:** Co-payment models do not work for this population. Full subsidy tied to income verification is required.

### 3. Indigenous-led mental health services — fund, don't refer

**Who:** Indigenous communities in all provinces

**What:** Fund Indigenous-designed and delivered mental health programs directly through Indigenous health authorities and community organisations, with multi-year stable funding.

**Why this is different from what exists:** Most provinces currently offer a referral pathway to mainstream services. The data shows this does not work — the unmet need gap persists at 4.6x. Mainstream services are not culturally safe for many Indigenous clients, and the gap will not close until communities have the resources to build their own.

**What to fund:** Land-based healing programs, Indigenous counsellors within communities, trauma-informed cultural care, and crisis response that does not involve police.

---

## What This Analysis Cannot Tell Us

- Whether mental health conditions are improving or worsening over time (single CCHS cycle)
- Patient-level pathways through the system and reasons for unmet need
- Cost-effectiveness of specific interventions at provincial scale

Longer-term trend analysis would require access to multiple CCHS cycles. Program-level cost-effectiveness requires administrative health data linkage through provincial data sharing agreements.
